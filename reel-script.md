# Reel Script: "Build Websites That Actually Hit"
**Format**: 3–5 min long-form reel (YouTube Shorts extended / Instagram / LinkedIn)
**Tone**: Confident, personal, artistically grounded, technically credible — timeless foundation

---

## SECTION 1 — HOOK

**[SCENE: Tight shot on your face, slightly side-lit, no intro music yet — silence creates tension]**

**[TEXT OVERLAY — appears word by word, white sans-serif on dark]**
> *"You can tell AI to build your website from scratch."*
> *"It just won't give it a soul."*

**[SPOKEN — direct, unhurried]**
> "Every developer right now is using agents to ship faster. And that's useful. But if you've recently looked at an AI-generated site — including your own — and felt like... this looks like everybody else's. Like walking into yet another clothing store with the same muted colors and sleeves. Something feels off. Common. Not intimately yours to experience. And by the time you've spent hours iterating to get something you actually like, you're also out of tokens. Left with the guilt of ruining the planet. That's the real cost."

**[PAUSE. Look into camera.]**

> "And here's the thing — that's not happening because the AI is bad. It's happening because you're treating it like a vending machine that should magically read your mind. Instead of what it actually is — a teammate that needs briefing."

**[PAUSE. Look into camera.]**

> "Hi, I'm Nuzhat. I'm a PM at Microsoft's CoreAI Developer Division. I create and build experiences for developers globally. Today I'm giving you an intro to a universal process — the kind of thinking that existed before vibecoding became a thing, and will outlive it too."

**[VISUAL: Your name and title appear as a clean lower-third — no logo, no corporate feel — something typographic and personal]**
`Nuzhat Minhaz — PM, Microsoft CoreAI Developer Division`

## SECTION 2 — WHY THIS MATTERS

**[SCENE: Cut to a slightly wider frame, casual — seated or standing, feels like a conversation]**

**[TEXT OVERLAY — quote card, aesthetic: muted background, elegant serif font]**
> *"AI can build everything. But it doesn't have taste."*
> *— Gazi Jarin, Software Engineer, Google*

**[SPOKEN]**
> "Gazi Jarin said something I keep coming back to. AI can build everything — but it doesn't have taste."

> "And that's not because the models are weak. It's because *every system that's ever endured is built on principles*. Design has them. The web has them. These aren't trends — they're the constants underneath."

> "When you hand a blank prompt to an agent, you're starting from zero. So if you only ask it to build something for outcome x, it will simply make an endlessly scrollable page. Because it doesn't have context on what this system is supposed to achieve for your users, who your user even is, and why."

> "Some of you watching are here because you're inspired by the era when legendary developers wrote interfaces that were *art*. You want to build that way — where web interfaces are a gateway to broader technical realms. Others of you are here because you think breaking into tech means building websites — and honestly, if that's all you think tech is, then that's why your results feel hollow. I'm here to help you change that."

**[VISUAL: Split screen — left side: a generic AI-generated landing page (use a placeholder mockup). Right side: a beautifully crafted, intentional design from the legendary era. Label them: "Generated" vs "Designed"]**

**[SPOKEN]**
> "What I want to give you aren't shortcuts. They're the foundations. The principles that survive every shift in tooling, every new framework, every platform evolution."

## SECTION 3A — WHO ARE YOU BUILDING FOR?

**[SCENE: Transition with a hand-drawn style animation or tablet sketch appearing — aesthetic, not corporate]**

**[TEXT OVERLAY — large, centered, clean]**
> *"Every project starts here."*

**[SPOKEN]**
> "Before you write anything. Before you pick colors or decide on components — three questions."

**[VISUAL: Three questions appear sequentially, each with a subtle illustration or icon]**

```
1. Who is using this?
2. What should they feel?
3. What do they need to do?
```

**[SPOKEN]**
> "This isn't design theory. This is your brief. Everything else — layout, typography, interaction — those are just answers to these three questions."

## SECTION 3B — MOBILE FIRST

**[SCENE: Show a hand holding a phone, close-up — warm light, aesthetic]**

**[TEXT OVERLAY]**
> *"Start with the smallest screen."*

**[SPOKEN]**
> "Mobile-first design existed long before it became a buzzword. A phone in someone's hand is tactile, intimate. It's closer than a mouse click from across a desk. And that closeness is where taste lives."

> "When designers and developers actually cared about the craft — when there were competitions just for how something *felt* — this was the standard. That standard didn't get worse. We just forgot why it mattered."

**[VISUAL: A brief montage — 2–3 seconds each — of award-winning web designs from Awwwards or similar. Dreamy, not technical. Text appears: "The era of intentional design isn't over. You just have to return to it."]**

## SECTION 3C — WEB DESIGN PRINCIPLES (ON SCREEN)

**[SCENE: Cut to a clean aesthetic graphic — could be a digital blackboard, a tablet sketch, or an editorial-style layout]**

**[TEXT OVERLAY / FULL SCREEN GRAPHIC — these stay visible while you speak]**

```
Foundations of Design

◦ Visual Hierarchy    — guide the eye with intention
◦ Contrast            — create clarity through difference
◦ Alignment           — structure builds confidence
◦ Proximity           — connection through closeness
◦ White Space         — restraint is design too
◦ Consistency         — repetition creates recognition
◦ Typography          — voice without words
◦ Color               — emotion before everything else
◦ Accessibility       — design for edges, win the center
◦ Responsive Design   — 375px baseline. Always.
```

**[SPOKEN — relaxed, like you're walking through a gallery]**
> "These aren't suggestions. They're shared language between you and your user. Violate them and users leave — not because they understand what's wrong, but because something *feels* wrong."

## SECTION 3D — NORMAN'S DESIGN PRINCIPLES (ON SCREEN)

**[VISUAL: New full-screen graphic — different aesthetic treatment, maybe more structured/architectural]**

**[TEXT OVERLAY]**
> *"The Universal Laws — Don Norman's Principles"*
> *Applied by every system that's lasted.*

```
Core Principles of Design

◦ Affordance      — the design tells you how to use it
◦ Signifiers      — visible signals for every action
◦ Constraints     — prevent wrong moves before they happen
◦ Mapping         — controls connect to their real effects
◦ Feedback        — system tells you what happened
◦ Conceptual Model — the user's mental model matches reality
```

**[SPOKEN]**
> "Don Norman wrote about these in the 1980s. They applied then. They apply now. Every interface you trust — your phone, your apps, VSCode — was designed by someone who understood these principles. When you brief an agent using this language, your output becomes foundational instead of fashionable."

## SECTION 4 — PROMPTING VSCode AGENT MODE

**[SCENE: Screen recording begins — VSCode open, agent mode panel visible. Clean setup, dark theme, aesthetic font]**

**[TEXT OVERLAY — top of screen]**
> *"The Process."*

**[SPOKEN]**
> "Open VSCode and switch to Agent Mode. Most people skip straight to 'build me a website.' Don't do that."

> "Start by establishing the thinking. Before any code."

**[ON SCREEN: You type the following prompt — slowly enough to read]**

```
Prompt 1 — Brainstorm

ok so I'm building a personal portfolio for a creative technologist.
before we write a single line — help me think through this.

who's actually landing on this site? what should they feel when 
they do? what sections make sense given that audience and goal?

give me a one-sentence design intention we can use as a north star 
throughout the build.

as you go, flag every assumption you're making. I want to catch 
anything misaligned before it becomes a structural decision.
```

**[SPOKEN]**
> "You're collaborating. Not asking for a commodity. Let it think first."

**[PAUSE — agent responds on screen, assumptions visibly listed]**

**[SPOKEN]**
> "That single instruction — flag your assumptions — cuts debugging time in half. Because now it's telling you the premises out loud. You catch the misalignment before it becomes foundation."

> "Refine the response. Push back. Question it. Then move to planning."

**[ON SCREEN: You type Prompt 2]**

```
Prompt 2 — Planning

good. based on what we mapped out — give me a full frontend 
development plan.

constraints:
- minimal user input, no auth, no complex state management
- not optimizing for scale yet, but needs to be clean and fast
- mobile-first, always — 375px is our baseline
- aesthetic direction: clean, editorial, warm

I need: file + folder structure, component breakdown with 
responsibilities, tech stack recommendation with your reasoning, 
and a color palette rationale tied back to the design intention.

no code yet. just the plan.
```

**[SPOKEN]**
> "Planning before building. It's not revolutionary. It's foundational. Every architect does this. So do you."

## SECTION 5 — TREATING THE AGENT AS AN ASSISTANT

**[SCENE: Back to face-cam — relaxed, conversational]**

**[SPOKEN]**
> "Here's what people don't talk about: agents work from patterns. They fill gaps. And sometimes they fill them wrong. That's expected. That's what collaborative means."

**[VISUAL: Screen recording — agent produces plan with an assumption highlighted. You respond:]**

```
"You assumed I wanted a contact form. 
I actually want a 'book a call' CTA that 
links to Calendly. Can you revise section 3 
of the plan with that in mind?"
```

**[SPOKEN]**
> "You're not correcting it because it failed. You're correcting it because *you know your user and your intention better than any system can*. That's the work. That's the taste."

> "Give it space to be wrong. Verbalize the assumptions back. Watch it course-correct. That's not debugging — that's collaboration."

**[TEXT OVERLAY — clean, centered]**
> *"The agent is your tool. You are the architect."*

Beyond the surface:
